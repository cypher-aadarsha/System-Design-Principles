The Core Challenge: How to Build?

Imagine we want to build a new "Super App". We have two main blueprints we can follow. Let's explore them using analogies we all know.

Approach 1: The Monolith

Everything under one giant roof.

Approach 2: Microservices

A collection of specialized shops.

The Monolith: The Bhat-Bhateni Model

A monolith is a single, unified application. All its parts are interconnected and run as one unit.

Analogy: A Bhat-Bhateni Superstore

One enormous building. Groceries, electronics, clothing, and the food court are all departments sharing the same foundation, security, and power supply. They are all part of "Bhat-Bhateni".

Monolithic "SuperApp"

User Accounts
E-commerce
Ride-Sharing
Bill Payments
Shared Database

Everything is tightly coupled in one large application.

The Bhat-Bhateni Model: Pros & Cons

Advantages

  • Simple Start: Easy to design and build the first branch.
  • Easy Testing: Just walk through the whole store to check everything.
  • Simple Deployment: Just "open the doors" of the one building.

Disadvantages

  • Scaling Issues: If the grocery section is packed for Dashain, you can't just expand it. You have to expand the whole building.
  • Single Point of Failure: A generator problem (‡§¨‡§§‡•ç‡§§‡•Ä ‡§ó‡§Ø‡•ã!) shuts down the entire store.
  • Slow to Update: Renovating the clothing section requires careful planning that might disrupt the whole store.

Microservices: The Labim Mall Model

A microservice architecture breaks an application into many small, independent services.

Analogy: A Modern Shopping Mall (like Labim or Civil Mall)

A collection of independent shops. Levis, QFX Cinemas, and the Food Court are all separate businesses. They can redecorate, hire staff, or even close without affecting the other shops.

Microservice "SuperApp"

UserService.api
ShopService.api
RideService.api
PaymentService.api

Each service is an independent application that communicates with others.

How Do The Shops Talk?

If the shops are separate, how do they work together? They need to communicate.

üìû Direct Call (API)

One shop calls another directly for an answer.

Analogy: QFX calls the pizza place: "Do you have a large pizza?" and waits for a "Yes/No". It's fast, but QFX is stuck waiting for the answer.

üì¨ Message Board (Queue)

One shop leaves a message for another to see later.

Analogy: Daraz posts an order on a notice board. A delivery partner (like a Pathao rider) sees the notice, picks up the package, and delivers it. Daraz doesn't wait; it can process the next order immediately.

The Labim Mall Model: Pros & Cons

Advantages

  • Independent Scaling: If QFX is sold out, only that service needs more resources.
  • Resilience: If the pizza place closes, you can still go shopping or watch a movie.
  • Tech Freedom: The cinema uses projectors; a store uses cash registers. Best tool for each job.

Disadvantages

  • Complex Management: Coordinating 100 shops is harder than one big store.
  • Network Overhead: Customers have to walk between shops (services talk over a network).
  • Data Consistency: Getting total sales for the whole mall is complex.

Case Study: A Nepali Digital Wallet

Let's think about an app like eSewa or Khalti.

As a Monolith:

A bug in the "Movie Tickets" feature could potentially make "Bank Transfers" fail. During Tihar, to handle the rush of `bhai tika` payments, you must scale the entire app - including the movie ticket part!

As Microservices:

The `MovieTicketService` is separate. If it fails, bank transfers still work perfectly. For Tihar, you can pour all your resources into scaling only the `BankTransferService` and `WalletService`. It's cheaper and more reliable.

The Journey of Pathao & Daraz

Most successful large-scale apps don't start with microservices. They evolve.

1.
Start as a Monolith: Pathao starts with just bike rides. It's one simple app. Easy to build and launch quickly.
2.
Feel the Pain: They add cars, food delivery, and groceries. The app becomes huge and slow. A bug in "Food" affects "Rides".
3.
Evolve to Microservices: They slowly break pieces off. A `RideService`, a `FoodService`, a `PaymentService`. Now, different teams can work on each part independently and innovate faster.

So, Which Blueprint to Use?

Choose Monolith (Bhat-Bhateni) When...

  • You have a small team or are just starting.
  • You are building a prototype (MVP).
  • Your idea is simple.

Choose Microservices (Labim Mall) When...

  • Your app is large and complex (like Daraz).
  • You need extreme reliability and scalability.
  • You have multiple large teams.

Conclusion

There is no single "best" answer. The right architecture depends on your specific needs.

Starting with a simple Monolith and evolving towards Microservices as you grow is a proven and powerful strategy.
